Voter Engagement and Voting Rights

Among the foundations and nonprofits, I worked with, it was often said that the resources they had were only a fraction of what government could potentially provide.

The long-term success of any nonprofit organization depended on a partnership with a responsive government, both representative and accountable to the communities they served. In other words, a healthy and functioning democracy.

In the 1990s, this trend was going in the wrong direction. Voter turnout hovered at historic lows. More than half of eligible voters didn’t vote in midterms, and even fewer voted in local elections. Most of the absent voters were low-income, young, or first time voters new to the political process.

If the nonprofit sector wanted to strengthen democracy and have their voices heard, one way was to help the people they served to vote.

However, at the time many nonprofits mistakenly believed that their charitable status meant they couldn’t talk to their clients and constituents about voting. The IRS never said that. The Treasury Department only said in a single sentence that 501c3 nonprofits, which most nonprofits are, cannot support or oppose candidates for public office. A sentence was added in the 1950s by then Senator Lyndon Johnson to protect educational and charitable 501c3 nonprofits from the quicksand of partisan politics, thus safeguarding the tax deductions for supporters and their ability to receive foundation and government grants. IRS guidance clearly stated that public 501c3 nonprofits may do any amount of voter registration and education, including helping people participate and vote, as long as they did so on a nonpartisan basis.

The nonprofit sector had largely left untapped their inherent capacity — as trusted messengers, deeply rooted in their local communities — to increase voter participation among the people they served and engaged. They have day-to-day personal contact with potential voters often missed by candidates and campaigns more narrowly focused on attracting already likely voters. What’s more, their non-partisan approach tends to work better with new voters seeking help and information.

BostonVOTE, MassVOTE

After a mid-career year at the Harvard Kennedy School focused on electoral reform, I had my first chance to work at a democracy organization, the Boston-based Commonwealth Coalition. The coalition, originally set up to help progressive reform-oriented candidates run for office, added three nonpartisan programs: one challenging the role of money in state politics, one encouraging people to vote in local elections, and the other to support women’s political leadership. In 1993, I was recruited to run the money and politics program.

One of my first calls was from Emerson College student Malia Lazu, about her effort to hold a concert for democracy on Boston Common, the city’s downtown public park. Malia had heard about the concert I’d helped produce, headlined by Bob Marley, to benefit freedom movements in South Africa. When the city turned down her request for a permit because it feared problems with a multiracial audience, she volunteered to work for us at the coalition, and was soon directing the “Voter Power” initiative.

We shared our disillusion with the state of politics, particularly its troubled history of racial bias. Too often, election outcomes were decided in primaries in districts designed to favor mostly white incumbents. On Election Day voters had few, if any, choices on the ballot, and too little reason to vote.

Malia and I met with Boston nonprofit and foundation leaders about a pilot program specifically focused on training nonprofits to integrate nonpartisan voter and civic participation into programs they already offered. We hoped that this civic venture would bring higher turnout and might encourage more candidates to run, despite the odds, resulting in more competitive elections.

Voter Power became Boston VOTE. Starting in Boston, we recruited a board of nonprofit leaders from the city’s lower turnout neighborhoods, including Kim Janey who, two decades later, would become the city’s first African-American and woman mayor — herself a reflection of a changing political landscape.

Boston VOTE grew on the strength of its nonprofit outreach. By our second year, we had signed up more than 120 nonprofits serving the low turnout neighborhoods, including Dorchester, Roxbury, Mattapan, and Allston/Brighton. Boston VOTE reached out to service providers, neighborhood development organizations, advocacy organizations, community health centers, and the like to offer our nonpartisan voter engagement training.

We made a special effort to engage youth through our own Young Civic Leaders program which offered paid internships for high school students to work with local nonprofits and scheduled time to learn about lawmaking at the state house and city hall.

Many of these early partners developed their own voting initiatives, including:

  • Dunk the Vote: A multi-faceted civic empowerment group that had its start registering young black voters at youth basketball competitions. They went on to partner with us on a voter registration canvass called “Knock Across Boston.”

  • Mass Council for Human Service Providers: A founding partner, it had its own Care VOTE program to encourages human services employees, clients, consumers, board members and friends to register and vote.

  • Chinese Progressive Association (CPA): CPA tripled neighborhood turnout in its first city election. It became an advocate for fair redistricting and translation for ballots and for city hall hearings so all residents could fully participate.

  • Codman Square Health Center: One of the first health centers to participate in voter engagement. It used dedicated volunteers and staff to offer voter registration on site and at community events.

  • Madison Park Community Development Corporation:It hosted Rox Vote, a coalition of Roxbury-based organizations to help residents register and vote, host candidate forums, and promote youth civic leadership.

  • Mass Immigration and Refugee Advocacy Coalition: MIRA registered new voters at citizenship ceremonies and supported civic engagement and advocacy in partnership with its 130 organizational members, many of which also worked with MassVOTE.

  • Chelsea Human Services: An early partner, it organized community canvasses to promote voting and recruited young poll workers. Today the city’s large Latino population is fully represented on its city council and school committee.

  • Boston Election Department: We partnered on efforts to provide voter education in lower turnout areas and eventually came to sit on their community advisory board.

A Civic Uprising

More Voters

  • Turnout rose in city and state elections for each of our first five years in the 12 Boston neighborhoods with historically lowest voter participation.

  • Turnout growth averaged 30 percentage points compared to only 4% in the neighborhoods with already higher turnout.

More Candidates

As turnout rose in the city’s more diverse neighborhoods, so did the diversity of candidates. For the first time, in 2004 Boston elected a Latino candidate, Felix D. Arroyo, for a City Council at-large seat. The Boston Globe called it “an unprecedented leap in voting by minorities in Boston.”

The next year, Cape Verdean candidate Andrea Cabral became Boston’s sheriff. “Coming on the heels of last year’s victory by city councilor Arroyo, it signals a shifting landscape in Boston’s voting public. … a new era in Boston politics.” –Boston Herald

BostonVOTE Becomes MassVOTE

As interest grew in other cities such as Brockton, Lowell, Lawrence, and Worcester, we changed our name to MassVOTE and expanded our work statewide on voting and voting rights.

  • Civic Engagement Initiative: A collaboration, first with Boston foundations, grew to make grants to nonprofits for voter outreach in several metro areas in every region. Nonprofit participants tracked the people they contacted to evaluate their impact on turnout of new and infrequent voters.

  • Census and Redistricting: A collaboration with our largest statewide partners to increase census participation and monitor drawing of new election districts.

  • Voter Access and Fair Elections: In 2000, Massachusetts lacked options many states already had to vote early, use online registration, or let voters correct an error in their registration when they went to vote. To address these issues, we joined with nonpartisan organizations like the League of Women Voters, Common Cause, the Lawyers for Civil Rights, and the Mass Public Interest Group to form what became the Election Modernization Coalition. For more see a timeline of our recent election policy history and achievements

  • Taking on Racial Gerrymandering in Boston and other Cities: We weren’t lawyers, and had no plans to make our first foray into voting rights with a lawsuit, but when confronted with evidence of racial gerrymandering we took on the challenge. We had recently decided to purchase newly available redistricting software in order to monitor the make-up of the new 2002 district lines which the legislature was about to draw for Congress and themselves. We invited an attorney from the Lawyers for Civil Rights Boston to help us discuss its impact on the Boston neighborhoods where we were working. The story became one of racial gerrymandering in Boston, a federal lawsuit by black voters, the indictment and disbarment of the House speaker and, ultimately, justice for the targeted communities. (For the full story.)

  • We continued our collaboration with t he Lawyers Committee to challenge biased voting methods in Springfield and Lowell. For decades their winner-take-all at-large voting method allowed a small group of white voters to retain full control of the City Council and School Committee. As a result, voters in a few neighborhood wards determined the outcome of most all municipal seats, who got hired for city jobs, and which neighborhoods received services. In both cities, the plaintiffs we represented won their case for fair elections. (For more add link)
