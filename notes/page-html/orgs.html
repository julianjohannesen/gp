After ending my first job out of college as a tax preparer for H&R Block, a call from my mother about a youth-run foundation to support activism in San Francisco sparked the idea of organizing my peers to try the same in Boston.

I had learned something about organizing from my activist mom. I’d also picked up some organizing principles volunteering evenings at a free tax clinic sponsored by Chelsea Fair Share. Its leaders, Mark and Barbara Splain, taught me the value of offering a service as part of earning trust and a chance to meet people who might join your cause. In their case an organization to build power among Chelsea’s low-income residents. It was a lesson I took with me for the rest of my career.

Philanthropy Years – Change not Charity

The Haymarket Foundation and the Funding Exchange

Having grown up more as a volunteer than a leader of campaigns, my first organizing project was the Haymarket People’s Fund. I started with advice from local faith programs that already made grants to community empowerment projects. Their unique approach included actively engaging members of the community in the awarding of grants. From there, I drafted a plan to create a foundation as my final paper for a degree in American Studies from Goddard College, where I had been studying. The course would also provide the name, a cross between the historic Haymarket Chicago protest for the eight-hour workday and Boston’s 300-year-old food market, with the same name.

Haymarket and the other alternative community funds that would form across the country sought to create a new generation of donors to support social change philanthropy. Under the banner of “Change not Charity,” we looked to invest in local organizations addressing the root causes of problems and taking steps towards more systemic and lasting solutions for the issues of the day. We hoped our grants would give visibility and credibility that could result in funding from the more established foundations. Often that was the case.

Among those with inherited wealth that came of age in the social movements of the ’60s and ’70s, there was interest in funding those movements—but many of them wanted anonymity, so the challenge was to find them. In line with the idea of first offering a service, we found success in organizing gatherings for people with family wealth; offering a safe environment to discuss the personal issues related to coming into inherited wealth. A place to talk about impact on relationships, concerns about where their money came from, and how to have a conversation with their families or financial gatekeepers about using it to support activism.

Haymarket had its first local donor meetings in partnership with the American Friends Service Committee. The Quaker network’s own donors had also asked for a personal retreat like this. It was established that the meeting had the assurance of anonymity and there would be no fundraising on site. National gatherings began when our mentor, David Hunter of the Stern Family Fund, convened the donors he advised to meet on a personal basis. Over time, the private meetings grew and moved to different locations around the country. These meetings would come to include activist leaders and guests who could offer their perspectives on social impact grantmaking. Through our formative years, we sponsored workshops and retreats like this—locally and nationally—that generated spin-offs and like-minded initiatives that supported a national trend to social change grantmaking.

Along with the change in the kind of grants made came a shift in power of who made them. We made a break from the private trustee model of grantmaking, where grants were approved by a board of family members or related legal and financial professionals, administered by a family office. We opted instead to have funding boards reflect the issues and communities where the grants were being made. It seemed more effective to have local leaders with grassroots organizing experience lead the process, and it was more in line with our values to have the grantmaking process itself be an extension of community empowerment.

In forming Haymarket’s founding board, we included activists like clerical worker Karen Nussbaum, who founded the organization 9to5, later made famous by the movie of the same name and Dolly Parton song. Another member was Walter Porter, the community-based chair of a Boston association for minority firefighters. Robert Zevin, a lifelong activist and pioneer in the field of socially responsible investing, was also involved. Ann Froines, advocate for women leaders, joined our board. This model gave activists the opportunity to be donors, and donors a chance to engage with those they supported. Other public foundations like ours that started later with a similar mission also opted for this approach.

In another break with traditional philanthropy, we went public. At the time only the United Way had TV ads and no one had a website. Because we were new and had to find new donors to join in our cause, we turned to marketing and outreach. It meant having a presence at foundation gatherings. It gave birth to the publication Robin Hood Was Right, inspired by and edited by author Adam Hochschild. The book, published in 1977 featured our funds and attracted a national audience with appearances on the Oprah Winfrey and Phil Donahue shows and coverage from the New York Times to Parade magazine, Town and Country, and others.

In the wake of the publicity, young donors started 15 similar community foundations in areas like Atlanta, New York, Chicago, Minneapolis, and one in Los Angeles, Liberty Hill Foundation, founded by my sister Sarah Pillsbury. As we grew, it became clear that we would benefit from an association. We created a national consortium called the Funding Exchange to support the growth of the local funds and to advance the conversation with other foundations around social change philanthropy.

This is not the history of a single foundation. It is the story of many similar efforts that built a movement to reshape philanthropy and build a community of foundations that shared a common social justice mission. Together we promoted the benefits of strategic grantmaking that sought to promote longer-term systemic approaches to the issues of the day.

Voter Engagement and Voting Rights

Among the foundations and nonprofits I worked with, it was often said that the money they had to address social issues was only a fraction of what government could do.

The long-term success of these organizations depended on a partnership with responsive government representatives who were accountable to the communities they served. In other words, a healthy and functioning democracy.

In the 1990s, this trend was going in the wrong direction. Voter turnout hovered at historic lows. More than half of eligible voters didn’t vote in midterms and fewer in local elections, most notably voters that were low-income, young, or relatively new citizens.

If the nonprofit sector wanted to strengthen democracy and have their voices heard, it had to do its part to make sure that the people they served voted.

Most nonprofits, however, mistakenly believed that their charitable status meant they couldn’t talk to their clients and constituents about voting. While private foundations faced some limitations, the IRS clearly stated that all public 501c3 charities could do any amount of voter registration and education, including helping people participate and vote. As long as they did so on a nonpartisan basis.

The nonprofit sector had largely left untapped their inherent capacity to increase voter participation among those voters with a history of low voter turnout. They are already trusted messengers, deeply rooted in their local communities. They have day-to-day personal contact with potential voters often missed by candidates and campaigns more narrowly focused on attracting already likely voters. What’s more, their non-partisan approach tends to work better with new voters seeking the help and information needed to be a voter.

BostonVOTE, MassVOTE

After a mid-career year at the Harvard Kennedy School focused on electoral reform, I had my first chance to work at a democracy organization, the Boston based Commonwealth Coalition. The coalition, originally set up to help progressive reform-oriented candidates run for office, added programs challenging the role of money in state politics and encouraging people to vote in local elections. I was recruited to run the money and politics program.

One of my first calls was from an Emerson College student, Malia Lazu, about her effort to hold a concert for democracy on the Boston Common, the city’s downtown public park. Malia had heard about the benefit concert, headlined by Bob Marley, for liberation movements in Southern Africa that I helped produce while working at Haymarket foundation. When the city turned down her request for a permit because it feared problems with a multiracial audience, she volunteered to work for democracy at the Coalition, and was soon directing the “Voter Power” initiative.

We shared our disillusion with the state of politics. Particularly its troubled history of racial bias, where election outcomes were decided in primaries in districts designed to favor mostly white incumbents. On Election Day voters had few, if any, choices on the ballot, and too little reason to vote.

Malia and I met with Boston nonprofit and foundation leaders about a pilot program specifically focused on training nonprofits to integrate voter and civic participation into programs they already offered on a nonpartisan basis. A civic venture we hoped would bring higher turnout and might encourage more candidates to run, despite the odds, making elections more competitive.

Voter Power became Boston VOTE. Starting in Boston, it recruited a board of nonprofit leaders from 18 of its lower turnout neighborhoods. One was Kim Janey who, two decades later, would become the city’s first African-American and woman mayor, herself a reflection of a changing Boston.

By our second year we had signed up more than 120 nonprofits serving the low turnout neighborhoods. Boston VOTE reached out to service providers, neighborhood development organizations, advocacy organizations, community health centers and the like. Each nonprofit received voter engagement training focused on voter registration, helping people become voters, and strategies to integrate voter outreach into ongoing programs while remaining nonpartisan.

Boston VOTE grew on the strength of its nonprofit outreach and these early partners that developed their own voter engagement initiatives, including

  • Dunk the Vote: A multi-faceted civic empowerment group, that had it start registering young black voters at youth basketball competitions set up for that purpose. They would partner with us on a voter registration canvass call Knock Across Boston.

  • Mass Council for Human Service Providers: A founding partner, it has its own Care VOTE program to encourages human services employees, clients, consumers, board members and friends to register and vote.

  • Chinese Progressive Association (CPA): CPA tripled neighborhood turnout in its first city election. It became an advocate for fair redistricting and translation for ballots and for city hall hearings so all residents could fully participate.

  • Codman Square Health Center: One of the first health centers to do voter engagement. It used dedicated volunteers and staff to do voter registration on site and at community events.

  • Madison Park Community Development Corporation: It hosted Rox Vote, a coalition of Roxbury-based organizations to help residents register and vote, host candidate forums, and promote youth civic leadership.

  • Mass Immigration and Refugee Advocacy Coalition: MIRA registered new voters at citizenship ceremonies and supported civic engagement and advocacy in partnership with its 130 organizational members, many that also worked with MassVOTE.

  • Chelsea Human Services: An early partner, it organized community canvasses to promote voting and recruited young poll workers. Today the city’s large Latino population is fully represented on the city council and school committee.

  • Boston Election Department: We partnered on efforts to do voter education in lower turnout areas and eventually came to sit on their community advisory board.

A Civic Uprising

More Voters

  • Turnout rose in city and state elections for all five of our first years in the 12 Boston neighborhoods that had the lowest participation in the past.

  • Turnout growth averaged 30 percentage points compared to only 4% in the neighborhoods with already higher turnout.

More Candidates

As turnout rose in the city’s more diverse neighborhoods, the diversity of candidates rose too. For the first time, in 2004 Boston elected a Latino candidate citywide for a City Council at-large seat. “An unprecedented leap in voting by minorities in Boston.” –The Boston Globe

The next year, a black Cape Verdean candidate became Boston’s sheriff. “Coming on the heels of last year’s victory by city councilor Arroyo, it signals a shifting landscape in Boston’s voting public. … a new era in Boston politics.” –Boston Herald

BostonVOTE Becomes MassVOTE

As interest grew in other cities like as Brockton, Lowell, Lawrence, and Worcester expanded our name change to MassVOTE. We added new programs in Boston and policy work statewide on voting and voting rights.

New Programs

  • Youth Civic Leadership: Paid internships for Boston youth high school students to work with local nonprofits, visit the state house and city council, and receive leadership training.

  • Civic Engagement Initiative: A collaboration with Boston foundations that made grants to nonprofits for voter outreach, and tracked who they personally contacted to evaluate their impact on turnout of new and infrequent voters.

  • Census and Redistricting: A collaboration with our largest statewide partners to increase census participation and later monitor creation of new election districts.

Voter Access and Fair Elections

In 2000, Massachusetts lagged behind many states that already offered good options to vote early or by mail, use online registration, or take advantage of same day registration, which let voters update their registration when they went to vote.

We worked in partnership with the other nonpartisan organizations—League of Women Voters, Common Cause, The Lawyers Committee, Mass Public Interest Group—to form what became the Election Modernization Coalition. For more see a timeline of our recent election policy history and achievements

The Voting Rights Lawsuits

Racial gerrymandering in Boston

We weren’t lawyers, and had no plans to make our first foray into voting rights with a lawsuit, but when confronted with evidence of racial gerrymandering we took on the challenge.

We had recently decided to purchase newly available redistricting software in order to monitor the make-up of the new 2002 district lines the legislature was about to draw for Congress and themselves. We invited an attorney from the Lawyers Committee for Civil Rights (now the Lawyers Committee) to help us discuss its impact on the Boston neighborhoods where we were working.

The story became one of racial gerrymandering in Boston, a federal lawsuit by black voters, the indictment and disbarment of the House speaker and, ultimately, justice for the targeted communities. (For the full story)

Challenging the bias of all At-Large Voting in Springfield and Lowell

We continued our collaboration with the Lawyers Committee to challenge biased voting methods in Springfield and Lowell. For decades their winner-take-all at-large voting method allowed a small group of white voters to retain full control of the City Council and School Committee. As a result, voters in a few neighborhood wards determined the outcome of most all municipal seats, who got hired for city jobs, and which neighborhoods received services. In both cities, the plaintiffs we represented won their case for fair elections. (For more add link)

Nonprofit VOTE

In the Beginning

In 2005, it was not my intention to nationally replicate what MassVOTE had done in Massachusetts. The idea came from the Minnesota Council of Nonprofits’ Marcia Avner and Jon Pratt, who had run a similar program the year before. The timing worked well. At the same time, Catalina Ruiz-Healy, of the San Francisco Tides Foundation, gave MassVOTE an unsolicited grant of $50,000 to start similar initiatives in Oakland and other states. A similar grant came from a donor at the Funding Exchange to support nonprofit-led voter outreach in New Mexico and Pittsburgh.

What soon made it possible to reach out to the nonprofit sector nationally was a $100,000 renewable grant from Geri Mannion, Managing Director of Strengthening U.S. Democracy at the Carnegie Corporation foundation. Geri, already a supporter of the MN Council of Nonprofits, had long wanted an initiative to tap into what she saw as the “sleeping giant” of the growing nonprofit sector to increase voter turnout among underrepresented communities and strengthen democracy. She believed, as we did, that harnessing the everyday contact that community-based nonprofits had with people less likely to vote could increase their likelihood to vote and encourage their family and friends to do the same.

Activating the Nonprofit Sector

With offices in Minnesota and Boston, we reached out to other state nonprofit convenors to see if they’d join what we then called the Nonprofit Voter Engagement Network or NVEN. At the same time, we started to develop a strategy for what it would take to reach and engage a national audience.

State Partners

Nonprofit associations in Michigan, Ohio, North Carolina, Maine, and Louisiana were among the first to join NVEN, followed by nonprofit networks in Arizona and Greater Pittsburgh. Every year, NVEN members started meeting yearly in a different state to hone our trainings and to strategize how we could grow the network nationally. Among the most memorable was the 2008 meeting held in New Orleans. We were inspired not only by the music and the city’s ongoing fight to recover from Hurricane Katrina, but by our irrepressible state host, Ashley Herad of the Louisiana Alliance for Nonprofits who made us take a knee at any hint of indecision to move forward

State Partner list as of 2022

As more states joined, a geographically diverse subset of state partners joined a field program to evaluate our approach and impact. Each partner identified and managed a cohort of community-based nonprofits in their state to register voters and have them sign a pledge to vote onsite or at their events. They would track who registered and/or signed a pledge to vote. The data allowed us to measure our impact and learn lessons to share in our webinars and trainings.

National Partners

Soon, our first national networks began to join, led by the Alliance for Strong Families and Communities, a long-standing national network of settlement houses, neighborhood centers, human service agencies, and grassroots organizations focused on economic empowerment and neighborhood engagement. The second was the National Association of Community Health Clinics. Initiated by its advocacy director, Marc Wetherhorn. It established the Community Health Vote Program, with voter engagement resources and guidance shared with its 1,200 community health centers. They were followed by national partners like Goodwill Industries, Feeding America, and YWCA, all agreeing to incorporate voter engagement into their communications strategies and to promote our monthly webinars and online resources to their local and state affiliates.

Our national growth accelerated with the support of sector leaders like Tim Delaney and the National Council of Nonprofits, which convened state associations in the 50 states, and Diana Aviv and the advocacy team at the Independent Sector (IS), with its membership of national nonprofit networks and foundations. It was at an IS conference that we met Steve Taylor, vice president of United Way Worldwide. He asked us to create a voter engagement toolkit tailored for local United Ways. Their director of advocacy and policy, Lindsay Torrico, joined our leadership council. Sector convenors like the Council on Foundations and the Regional Associations of Grantmakers (now called the United Philanthropy Forum) also joined. We saw the results, as more of their affiliates signed up for a webinar training, joined us on National Voter Registration Day, and invited us to present at their national conference.

National Partner list as of 2022

Partnerships with Election Officials

In 2009, we were invited by the National Association of Secretaries of State and soon after the National Association of State Election Directors to be an official partner for the nonprofit sector. Their staff and members valued the nonpartisan guidance and training we gave nonprofits. We found our commitment to nonpartisanship opened doors, not just to the Secretaries of State, but to a wide variety of state and national partnerships and initiatives – the largest of which was National Voter Registration Day, and how we became one of the event’s founders and its primary manager.

National Voter Registration Day: A Celebration of Democracy

In 2012, Nonprofit VOTE joined the leading nonpartisan voter registration organizations to establish National Voter Registration Day. Celebrated on the fourth Tuesday of every September, it quickly gained momentum as a nonpartisan civic holiday celebrating our democracy. Each year, organizations, volunteers, and local election offices “hit the streets”—online and in the field—in a single day of coordinated field, technology, and media efforts. Support for the day comes from the largest nonpartisan registration groups, including media companies like Google, Reddit, MTV, Tumblr, and Facebook, and thousands of grassroots community partners. In 2020, more than 1.6 million voters registered or updated their registration on National Voter Registration Day. Along with the sponsors, 4,589 community partners signed up, recruiting 31,474 volunteers to conduct drives and stage events.

Trainings and Resources

The Trainings

When we began, nonprofits mainly knew what they couldn’t do around elections, but had little knowledge of what they could do on a nonpartisan basis to help people vote and educate candidates on their issues.

Our basic trainings explained the ban on partisan activities and when and how it started. The ban stems from a single sentence inserted into the tax code in 1954 by then Senator Lyndon Johnson. Johnson did so to protect 501c3 nonprofits from the quicksand of partisan politics, thus safeguarding the tax deductions allowed for donations to charitable and educational nonprofits for the long run. The IRS has repeatedly stated the ban did not exclude any non-partisan activity.

The trainings then highlighted the most common nonpartisan activities they could safely undertake, like voter registration, candidate forums, and reminders to vote. They also featured the benefits of voting for the nonprofit and the people they served, and offered strategies to help already busy organizations imagine how they could incorporate this work into their ongoing services, outreach, or events.

The Resources

Perhaps my greatest contribution in my years at Nonprofit VOTE was creating the stock materials and resources that became the foundation of our guidance and partner trainings. I had guidance from leading experts in the field, most notably our attorney John Pomeranz, and Greg Colvin, former co-chair of the American Bar Association Subcommittee on Political and Lobbying Activities of Exempt Organizations, as well as from the bolder advocacy program of the Alliance for Justice. Our online resources and monthly web trainings are accessed by nonprofits year round in all 50 states.

The Evaluation of Impact: Measuring How Nonprofits Help People Vote

Every year, Nonprofit VOTE sees an increase in the number of nonprofits signing up for its trainings and voter engagement on site and in their community. A 2020 survey by the Urban Institute found that one in five of the nation’s 875,000 registered nonprofits now do activities like voter registration and education.

But who are they reaching and is it helping increase voter participation? To find out, starting in 2012, Nonprofit VOTE enlisted a diverse set of state partners to manage a cohort of community-based nonprofits to track their voter engagement.

The participating nonprofits logged the people they registered to vote or who signed a voter pledge card and discussed their plan to vote. State voter files allowed us to do an independent analysis of the demographics and turnout rates of those we contacted to all other registered voters in the same area. The findings have consistently shown:

  1. The people the nonprofits reach are disproportionately younger (ages 18-25), lower income, and black, hispanic, or of color.

  2. T he voter turnout for those we contacted varies from 5-15 points higher for the nonprofit voters, compared to all others. Nonprofits have had their largest impact on raising turnout in midterms.

  3. Turnout increases are highest among those least expected to vote, based on their demographics and voting history.
