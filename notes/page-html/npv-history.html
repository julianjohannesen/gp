Nonprofit VOTE

In the Beginning

In 2005, it was not my intention to nationally replicate what MassVOTE had done in Massachusetts. The idea came from the Minnesota Council of Nonprofits’ Marcia Avner and Jon Pratt, who had run a similar program the year before. The timing worked well. At the same time, Catalina Ruiz-Healy, of the San Francisco Tides Foundation, gave MassVOTE an unsolicited grant of $50,000 to start similar initiatives in Oakland and other states. A similar grant came from a donor at the Funding Exchange to support nonprofit-led voter outreach in New Mexico and Pittsburgh.

What soon made it possible to reach out to the nonprofit sector nationally was a $100,000 renewable grant from Geri Mannion, Managing Director of Strengthening U.S. Democracy at the Carnegie Corporation foundation. Geri, already a supporter of the MN Council of Nonprofits, had long wanted an initiative to tap into what she saw as the “sleeping giant” of the growing nonprofit sector to increase voter turnout among underrepresented communities and strengthen democracy. She believed, as we did, that harnessing the everyday contact that community-based nonprofits had with people less likely to vote could increase their likelihood to vote and encourage their family and friends to do the same.

Activating the Nonprofit Sector

With offices in Minnesota and Boston, we reached out to other state nonprofit convenors to see if they’d join what we then called the Nonprofit Voter Engagement Network or NVEN. At the same time, we started to develop a strategy for what it would take to reach and engage a national audience.

State Partners

Nonprofit associations in Michigan, Ohio, North Carolina, Maine, and Louisiana were among the first to join NVEN, followed by nonprofit networks in Arizona and Greater Pittsburgh. Every year, NVEN members started meeting yearly in a different state to hone our trainings and to strategize how we could grow the network nationally. Among the most memorable was the 2008 meeting held in New Orleans. We were inspired not only by the music and the city’s ongoing fight to recover from Hurricane Katrina, but by our irrepressible state host, Ashley Herad of the Louisiana Alliance for Nonprofits who made us take a knee at any hint of indecision to move forward

State Partner list as of 2022

As more states joined, a geographically diverse subset of state partners joined a field program to evaluate our approach and impact. Each partner identified and managed a cohort of community-based nonprofits in their state to register voters and have them sign a pledge to vote onsite or at their events. They would track who registered and/or signed a pledge to vote. The data allowed us to measure our impact and learn lessons to share in our webinars and trainings.

National Partners

Soon, our first national networks began to join, led by the Alliance for Strong Families and Communities, a long-standing national network of settlement houses, neighborhood centers, human service agencies, and grassroots organizations focused on economic empowerment and neighborhood engagement. The second was the National Association of Community Health Clinics. Initiated by its advocacy director, Marc Wetherhorn. It established the Community Health Vote Program, with voter engagement resources and guidance shared with its 1,200 community health centers. They were followed by national partners like Goodwill Industries, Feeding America, and YWCA, all agreeing to incorporate voter engagement into their communications strategies and to promote our monthly webinars and online resources to their local and state affiliates.

Our national growth accelerated with the support of sector leaders like Tim Delaney and the National Council of Nonprofits, which convened state associations in the 50 states, and Diana Aviv and the advocacy team at the Independent Sector (IS), with its membership of national nonprofit networks and foundations. It was at an IS conference that we met Steve Taylor, vice president of United Way Worldwide. He asked us to create a voter engagement toolkit tailored for local United Ways. Their director of advocacy and policy, Lindsay Torrico, joined our leadership council. Sector convenors like the Council on Foundations and the Regional Associations of Grantmakers (now called the United Philanthropy Forum) also joined. We saw the results, as more of their affiliates signed up for a webinar training, joined us on National Voter Registration Day, and invited us to present at their national conference.

Partnerships with Election Officials

In 2009, we were invited by the National Association of Secretaries of State and soon after the National Association of State Election Directors to be an official partner for the nonprofit sector. Their staff and members valued the nonpartisan guidance and training we gave nonprofits. We found our commitment to nonpartisanship opened doors, not just to the Secretaries of State, but to a wide variety of state and national partnerships and initiatives – the largest of which was National Voter Registration Day, and how we became one of the event’s founders and its primary manager.

National Voter Registration Day: A Celebration of Democracy

In 2012, Nonprofit VOTE joined the leading nonpartisan voter registration organizations to establish National Voter Registration Day. Celebrated on the fourth Tuesday of every September, it quickly gained momentum as a nonpartisan civic holiday celebrating our democracy. Each year, organizations, volunteers, and local election offices “hit the streets”—online and in the field—in a single day of coordinated field, technology, and media efforts. Support for the day comes from the largest nonpartisan registration groups, including media companies like Google, Reddit, MTV, Tumblr, and Facebook, and thousands of grassroots community partners. In 2020, more than 1.6 million voters registered or updated their registration on National Voter Registration Day. Along with the sponsors, 4,589 community partners signed up, recruiting 31,474 volunteers to conduct drives and stage events.

Trainings and Resources

The Trainings

When we began, nonprofits mainly knew what they couldn’t do around elections, but had little knowledge of what they could do on a nonpartisan basis to help people vote and educate candidates on their issues.

Our basic trainings explained the ban on partisan activities and when and how it started. The ban stems from a single sentence inserted into the tax code in 1954 by then Senator Lyndon Johnson. Johnson did so to protect 501c3 nonprofits from the quicksand of partisan politics, thus safeguarding the tax deductions allowed for donations to charitable and educational nonprofits for the long run. The IRS has repeatedly stated the ban did not exclude any non-partisan activity.

The trainings then highlighted the most common nonpartisan activities they could safely undertake, like voter registration, candidate forums, and reminders to vote. They also featured the benefits of voting for the nonprofit and the people they served, and offered strategies to help already busy organizations imagine how they could incorporate this work into their ongoing services, outreach, or events.

The Resources

The Evaluation of Impact: Measuring How Nonprofits Help People Vote

Every year, Nonprofit VOTE sees an increase in the number of nonprofits signing up for its trainings and voter engagement on site and in their community. A 2020 survey by the Urban Institute found that one in five of the nation’s 875,000 registered nonprofits now do activities like voter registration and education.

But who are they reaching and is it helping increase voter participation? To find out, starting in 2012, Nonprofit VOTE enlisted a diverse set of state partners to manage a cohort of community-based nonprofits to track their voter engagement.

The participating nonprofits logged the people they registered to vote or who signed a voter pledge card and discussed their plan to vote. State voter files allowed us to do an independent analysis of the demographics and turnout rates of those we contacted to all other registered voters in the same area. The findings have consistently shown:

  1. The people the nonprofits reach are disproportionately younger (ages 18-25), lower income, and black, hispanic, or of color.

  2. T he voter turnout for those we contacted varies from 5-15 points higher for the nonprofit voters, compared to all others. Nonprofits have had their largest impact on raising turnout in midterms.

  3. Turnout increases are highest among those least expected to vote, based on their demographics and voting history.
